Learning Objectives

Introduction

In Part 1 of the Time Value of Money tutorial, you learned about the general format of the TVM equation. In Part 2, you learned about Compound Interest. In part 3, we added cash flows. Here in part 4 of the TVM tutorials, we'll learn about the Present Value of Annuities (PVA) to answer the question:
How much should I pay today for an annuity?

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you will:

  • Given a word problem, calculate PVA using the TVM equation, the PVA equation, a PVA Factor table, and a financial calculation
  • Calculate PVA with compounding using the four methods listed above
  • Compare annuities with different periods, interest rates, compounding periods, and cash flows to determine the best option

Requirements

To complete this tutorial, you will need:

  • Mastery of the basic TVM equation with compounding
  • Basic algebra skills, including the Order of Operations (PEMDAS) and FOIL
  • Scratch paper and pencil or pen
  • A calculator (optional: a financial calculator)

TVM may required you to use math skills you haven't used in a while. Please contact your instructor if you need additional help!

TVM: Annuities

Introduction

Then we introduced the idea of cash flows in the form of perpetuities and annuities

We also introduced two types of annuities based on the timing of payments:

  • Perpetuity: a series of equal-sized, regularly-scheduled payments paid indefinitly
  • Annuity: a series of equal-sized, regularly-scheduled payments over a specified, finite period of time
    • Ordinary Annuity: Payments are made at the end of each period
    • Annuity Due: Payments are made at the beginning of each period

Since perpetuities are rare, we focused largely on annuities. Specifically, we calculated the Future Value of Annuities (FVA) using the following equation:

FVA = The future value of the annuity
CF = amount of each payment
n = number of years
i = stated interest rate
m = number of compounding periods

This brings us up to the topic of this tutorial. We can calculate the Future Value of Annuities, but what about the Present Value of Annuities?

Performing the Algebra

Once you have plugged the variables into the equation, solve using the order of operations. Remember the acronym PEMDAS. Start with parentheses and then exponents. Then perform (from left to right) multiplication and division. Finally, add and subtract (from left to right).

We'll perform the calculations separately, starting with the annuity.

Annuity (PVA) Calculation

  • P: Since brackets function like parentheses, so we will first simplify the numbers in the parentheses and then work on the bracket as a whole.
  • E: Raise the number within the parentheses to the exponent.

  • MD: Simplify the embedded fraction.
  • AS: Subtract the numbers in the numerator.
  • MD: Simplify the equation within the bracket and multiply the CF by the number within the bracket.

Lump Sum (PV) Calculation

  • P: Start with the numbers in the parentheses. Since brackets function like parentheses, we will work in the following order: inner parentheses, outer brackers, and then outside the bracket.
  • E: Raise the number within the parentheses to the exponent.

  • MD: Divide both sides by the number in the parentheses to isolate PV. Then solve for PV.
  • AD: Add the Present Value of the annuity (PVA) and lump sum (PV) to find what the bond is worth today.
